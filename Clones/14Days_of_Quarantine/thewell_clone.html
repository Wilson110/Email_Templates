MARIYA RUSSELL is the FIRST Black woman to earn a coveted Michelin star. Russell was awarded the honor in September 2019 for her amazing culinary skills as the chef de cuisine of Chicago-based Japanese restaurants Kumiko and Kikko, where according to Eater, "she prepares and serves every dish on the seven course tasting menu".

NATANYA MONTGOMERY is the latest Black woman to raise at least $1 million for her startup. The CEO and founder of Naza Beauty, a haircare startup and salon opening its first location in San Francisco, raised a $1 million round led by Alexis Ohanian. The startup, a sort of Drybar for protective styles, utilizes proprietary software that learns a customer's styling preferences and makes product recommendations. The company also creates its own products based on data it gleans from its very own product development community of 10,000 Black and brown women.

STACY BROWN-PHILPOT, REDIET ABEBE and MONIQUE IDLETT-MOSLEY are three of the 17 women recognized by ELLE magazine as the future of tech. Brown-Philpot, CEO of TaskRabbit, was COO of the company before she was promoted to the top spot in 2016. Abebe, the FIRST Black woman to complete a PhD in computer science at Cornell, is an expert in AI and its failures to accurately represent people of color. And Idlett-Mosley is a powerhouse investor at Reign Ventures, a firm she co-founded in 2017 with the thesis of focusing on early-stage companies run by women and people of color.

RAMONA HOOD has become the FIRST Black woman to to lead a FedEx operating company. According to USA Today, Hood became the CEO of FedEx Custom Critical on January 1. Note: Hood is not the CEO of the company. It is common for large corporations to name individual CEOs to different lines of business.

HOW DO YOU STRIKE A BALANCE BETWEEN ACCURACY AND CREATIVITY WHEN WRITING HISTORICAL FICTION?

"I always always start with the moment in history I want to tell, and then create/imagine a story that will best allow me to tell the narrative. Writing historical fiction is interesting in its own right, but writing a historical fiction about the transatlantic slave as a Black woman added another layer. Other historical fiction writers call it “channeling.” That is, whoever you’re researching from the past is excited that you’ve decided to tell their story, and the words just flow..."
